TAJ MAHAL

The Epitome of Love and Architectural Brilliance.
The Taj Mahal, an ivory-white marble mausoleum on the south bank of the Yamuna river in Agra, was commissioned in 1632 by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan to house the tomb of his favorite wife, Mumtaz Mahal.
It also houses the tomb of Shah Jahan himself.
The Taj Mahal is renowned for its Mughal architecture, a blend of Indian, Persian, and Islamic styles.
Its central dome reaches a height of 73 meters (240 feet) and is flanked by four minarets, each more than 40 meters (130 feet) tall.

RED FORT

A Marvel of Mughal Architecture.
The Red Fort, located in the heart of Delhi, served as the main residence of the Mughal emperors for nearly 200 years. It was commissioned by Emperor Shah Jahan in 1638 when he decided to shift the capital from Agra to Delhi. The fort is renowned for its massive enclosing walls of red sandstone, which span over 2 kilometers. It showcases the architectural brilliance of the Mughal era with its intricate carvings, beautiful gardens, and grand halls. It serves as the location for the annual Independence Day celebrations where the Prime Minister hoists the national flag.

GATEWAY OF INDIA

An Iconic Monument of Mumbai.
It is an arch-monument built in the early 20th century to commemorate the landing of King George V and Queen Mary at Apollo Bunder during their visit to India in 1911.
Designed by the British architect George Wittet, it is a combination of Hindu and Muslim architectural styles.
The central dome is 48 feet in diameter and 83 feet high, symbolizing the grandeur of the British Empire.
The Gateway of India has witnessed several significant events, including the departure of the last British troops from India in 1948.
